\subsection{The Workload Gap: Agentic vs.\ Chat Saturation}
\label{sec:workload_gap}

Figure~\ref{fig:saturation} shows throughput-vs-concurrency saturation curves for Llama-3.1-8B (TP=1) on A100 under three representative workload profiles. The chat-short profile (ISL=500, OSL=300) saturates at approximately 920 output tokens/s near concurrency 40, while the chat-medium profile (ISL=2000, OSL=1000) saturates at approximately 450 tokens/s. On H100, the coding-agent profile (ISL=17K, OSL=800) saturates at substantially lower throughput due to prefill-dominated computation.

\subsection{Latency Profiles: TTFT and TPOT Under Load}
\label{sec:latency}

Beyond throughput, the latency characteristics of agentic workloads differ qualitatively from chat workloads. Figure~\ref{fig:latency} shows TTFT and TPOT at concurrency 1 (no queueing) and concurrency 40 (moderate load) across profiles.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    % TODO: Generate TTFT/TPOT comparison bar charts
    \fbox{\parbox{0.9\textwidth}{\centering\vspace{2cm}\textbf{Figure: TTFT and TPOT Across Workload Profiles}\\[4pt]
    \small Grouped bar chart: conc=1 vs conc=40 for chat-short, chat-medium, chat-long\\
    on A100 Llama-8B TP=1\vspace{2cm}}}
    \caption{Median TTFT (left) and TPOT (right) for Llama-3.1-8B on A100 at concurrency 1 and 40. TTFT scales linearly with ISL at low concurrency (dominated by prefill compute) but grows super-linearly under load as prefill requests compete for GPU compute. TPOT remains relatively stable across profiles at low concurrency but degrades under load due to reduced decode batch efficiency.}
    \label{fig:latency}
\end{figure}

At concurrency 1, TTFT scales approximately linearly with input sequence length---a direct consequence of the compute-bound prefill phase. At concurrency 40, TTFT grows super-linearly because concurrent prefill requests compete for limited GPU compute, creating queueing delays that compound with longer input sequences. TPOT is less sensitive to workload profile at low concurrency (decode is memory-bound regardless of input length) but degrades under high concurrency as the continuous batching scheduler interleaves prefill and decode operations.

\subsection{Dense vs.\ MoE Architecture Comparison}
\label{sec:dense_moe}

Mixtral-8$\times$7B (MoE, 8 experts, top-2) and Llama-70B (dense) have comparable total parameter counts but fundamentally different execution profiles. On A100 with TP=4, Mixtral achieves higher throughput on short chat workloads (each token activates only 2 of 8 expert FFN blocks) but shows less advantage on long-context agentic workloads where prefill attention---shared across both architectures---dominates.

\paragraph{Connecting kernels to serving performance.}
The roofline analysis explains the saturation behavior observed in Section~\ref{sec:workload_gap}. Chat-short requests (ISL=500) spend the majority of per-request GPU time in memory-bound decode kernels, which scale linearly with batch size under continuous batching---enabling high throughput. Chat-long and coding-agent requests shift GPU time to compute-bound prefill GEMMs that cannot be batched as efficiently (each request's prefill must complete before decode begins), creating a throughput bottleneck at lower concurrency.
