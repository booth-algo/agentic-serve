\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
We benchmark 6 LLMs , with parameters spanning from 8B to 120B. We covered dense (Llama3.1-8B, Qwen3.5-9B,Qwen3.5-27B) and mixture of expert architectures (Mixtral-8x7B, gpt-oss-20b, gpt-oss-120b). Inference was served on H100-SXM5-80GB GPUs and A100-SXM4-40GB GPUs using vLLM v0.19 and SGlang in bf16 precision with prefix caching. Concurrency was swept across 1 - 320 simultaneous requests across 16 workload profiles, issuing 50 requests after 3 warmup requests per configuration. We collect data on 3 different types of workloads : agent traces from SWE-Bench coding sessions (ISL = 17,000, OSL = 800), ShareGPT chat baselines (ISL <= 500, OSL <= 300), and synthetic stress tests isolating prefill workloads (ISL = 8192, OSL = 256). [Table] 4.1 reports on inference across these workloads, 4.2 traces kernel-level regime shifts, and 4.3 - 4.4 validate the prediction pipeline.


\subsection{Saturation Behavior Across Workload Profiles}
\label{sec:workload_gap}

Figure~\ref{fig:saturation} shows throughput-vs-concurrency curves for Llama-3.1-8B (TP=1) on A100 under three workload profiles. Chat-short (ISL=500, OSL=300) reaches 920 output tokens/s at concurrency 40 and plateaus. At concurrency 1, TPOT is 12.84\,ms (74 tok/s); at concurrency 320, TPOT rises to 16.13\,ms (910 tok/s), indicating near-complete saturation by concurrency 40.

Chat-medium saturates at approximately 450 tok/s. The coding-agent profile (ISL=17K, OSL=800, measured on H100) saturates at 240 tok/s---3.8$\times$ lower than chat-short. This gap persists across all model configurations (Table~\ref{tab:saturation}).

The mechanism is straightforward: longer input sequences increase the fraction of per-request GPU time spent in compute-bound prefill GEMMs, which cannot be overlapped as efficiently with decode under continuous batching. We quantify this at the kernel level in Section~\ref{sec:roofline}.

\subsection{Latency Under Load}
\label{sec:latency}

At concurrency 1 on H100, Llama-8B (TP=1) achieves TTFT=26.4\,ms and TPOT=6.26\,ms on the chat-medium profile. On A100 at the same concurrency, TPOT is 12.84\,ms for chat-short---a 2.05$\times$ ratio consistent with the H100's 1.6$\times$ HBM bandwidth advantage (3{,}352 vs 2{,}039 GB/s) for memory-bound decode.

    \fbox{\parbox{0.9\textwidth}{\centering\vspace{2cm}\textbf{[Figure: TTFT and TPOT Across Profiles and Concurrency]}\\[4pt]
    \small Grouped bar chart: conc=1 vs conc=40 for chat-short, chat-medium, chat-long\vspace{2cm}}}
    \caption{Median TTFT (left) and TPOT (right) for Llama-3.1-8B on A100 at concurrency 1 and 40. TTFT scales with ISL at low concurrency (prefill is compute-bound); at concurrency 40, TTFT grows super-linearly as prefill requests queue for GPU compute.}
    \label{fig:latency}
\end{figure}

TTFT scales with ISL at low concurrency because prefill is compute-bound: doubling the input length roughly doubles prefill time. At concurrency 40, TTFT grows super-linearly as concurrent prefill requests queue for GPU compute. TPOT is less sensitive to profile at low concurrency---decode is memory-bound regardless of input length---but degrades under load as the scheduler interleaves prefill and decode.

The prefill GEMM at $M$=512 achieves 49\% of the A100's 312 TFLOPS peak---compute-bound, with arithmetic intensity well above the ridge point. The decode GEMM at $M$=1 achieves 54\% of peak HBM bandwidth---memory-bound, as expected for a matrix-vector product. The MoE expert GEMM at $M$=64 remains memory-bound despite moderate batch size, achieving 65\% of peak bandwidth; the small per-expert batch prevents the kernel from crossing into the compute-bound regime.

\paragraph{Kernel count: dense vs.\ MoE.} A 2-layer forward pass of Llama-8B generates 419 kernel invocations. Mixtral-8$\times$7B generates 1{,}527---3.7$\times$ more---due to per-expert GEMM dispatch, routing logic, and gather/scatter operations. This kernel launch overhead is a distinct bottleneck for MoE serving that is invisible to throughput-level benchmarks.

\paragraph{Connecting kernels to serving throughput.}
Chat-short requests (ISL=500) spend most per-request GPU time in memory-bound decode kernels, which batch efficiently under continuous batching. Coding-agent requests (ISL=17K) shift GPU time to compute-bound prefill GEMMs. Prefill cannot be batched as aggressively---each request's prefill must complete before decode begins---creating the throughput ceiling observed in Section~\ref{sec:workload_gap}.
