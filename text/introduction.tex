\textit{How should we benchmark LLM inference when the dominant workload is a multi-step coding agent, not a short chat exchange?} LLM agents now power coding assistants and computer-use systems. Inference benchmarks still evaluate serving infrastructure with chat-scale single-turn traces, arbitrary synthetic lengths, and fixed operating points. The MLPerf Inference v5.1 LLM suite~\citep{mlperf_v51} covers summarisation and long-context reasoning, with workloads from 1{,}024-token Q\&A (Llama~2~70B on OpenOrca) up to 128K-token document tasks (Llama~3.1~405B). It remains single-turn with no multi-step tool use. The harnesses bundled with Kwon \etal{}'s vLLM~\citep{vllm} and Zheng \etal{}'s SGLang~\citep{sglang} default to ShareGPT~\citep{sharegpt} prompts clustered near 1{,}024 tokens at a fixed concurrency.

Benchmarking has three gaps. First, no public benchmark measures the agentic, multi-turn, tool-using regime that now dominates production: coding and computer-use agents issue long-context multi-turn requests that neither MLPerf, vLLM, nor SGLang exercises. Second, ISL and OSL are set by the benchmark author, not measured from a trace: InferenceX~\citep{inferencex} treats them as user-supplied knobs, and its random-token profiles match requested counts but not trace distributions. Third, every public serving-benchmark harness reports a single operating point: MLPerf Server~\citep{mlperf} fixes open-loop Poisson at a pre-chosen rate, vLLM and SGLang default to a fixed concurrency, and none sweeps closed-loop concurrency to saturation.

In this paper, we present \textsc{AgentServe}, a benchmark suite and analysis framework that addresses each of these problems. Our Tier-1 profiles are drawn from real agentic traces: SWE-Bench~\citep{swebench} coding agents (ISL\,$\approx$\,17{,}000 / OSL\,$\approx$\,800, 21.3:1 ratio) and TerminalBench and OSWorld multi-turn sessions of 2 to 1{,}000 turns. ShareGPT single- and multi-turn chat serve as Tier-2 baselines. We measure ISL and OSL distributions from these traces directly. Synthetic random-token profiles (decode-heavy, prefill-heavy, random-1k) are demoted to Tier-3 microbenchmarks that isolate a single regime rather than stand in for a deployed workload. We expose the saturation ceiling with a \textit{closed-loop concurrency sweep} from $C\,{=}\,1$ to $320$ on A100 and H100. An \texttt{asyncio.Semaphore(C)} admission gate keeps $C$ requests in flight, admitting the next only when one completes. Closed-loop concurrency matches production agent traffic, where the next request is gated by the previous completion in a tool-use loop, more faithfully than open-loop Poisson.

\textit{Concurrency} is the maximum number of in-flight requests under this closed-loop gate, in contrast to the open-loop Poisson arrival used in MLPerf Server. \textit{Request throughput} ($\,$req/s) is \textsc{AgentServe}'s headline capacity metric. We prefer it to \textit{output token throughput} ($\,$tok/s), which undersells the agentic gap at 21:1 ISL/OSL ratios, and to \textit{total token throughput}, which conflates prefill-bound input with decode-bound output. \textit{Saturation} is the concurrency $C^{\star}\,{=}\,\argmax_{C}\,\text{req/s}(C)$ at which request throughput ceases to rise with added concurrency (slope-floor $|d(\text{req/s})/dC|\,{<}\,\epsilon$). At $C^{\star}$, the serving pipeline itself is the bottleneck: KV-cache, prefill GEMMs, and scheduler queues~\citep{sarathi} admit no further concurrency, and adding requests only grows the queue. We prefer $C^{\star}$ to peak$\,$tok/s, which synthetic workloads inflate by reporting decoded tokens without ever saturating the pipeline. The TTFT-knee and KV-cache fill fraction corroborate $C^{\star}$ but do not replace it.

Consider Llama-3.1-8B on an H100: it sustains 9{,}327\,tok/s of output under a chat-short workload but only 4{,}904\,tok/s under a coding-agent workload at saturation, a 1.9$\times$ gap. The capacity gap is larger still, at 55.15\,req/s versus 15.03\,req/s, a 3.7$\times$ reduction in requests served per second. TTFT widens further. Single-turn agentic requests incur 26--468$\times$ longer time-to-first-token than chat, and multi-turn sessions 5--28$\times$, across configurations. Figure~\ref{fig:quadrant} shows Tier-1 and Tier-2 separating by 56$\times$ in median operational intensity with no IQR overlap, a qualitative regime shift rather than a quantitative nudge.

\textsc{AgentServe} makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Saturation benchmarks on real agentic workloads.} We sweep closed-loop concurrency from 1 to 320 across six models spanning three families (Llama-3, Qwen3.5, GPT-OSS; dense and MoE, 8B--120B total parameters) on H100 and A100 GPUs under vLLM, collecting 2{,}527 benchmark configurations (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}). Agentic workloads raise TTFT by 26--468$\times$ single-turn and 5--28$\times$ multi-turn, and cut request throughput by up to 3.7$\times$ against chat baselines.
    \item \textbf{Kernel-level roofline with OI/CF characterisation.} We profile $\sim$3{,}000 CUDA kernel invocations across 48 model-phase-batch configurations (Sec.~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline}). Prefill GEMMs reach 785\,TFLOPS on H100 (compute-bound); decode GEMMs operate at 2.9\,TFLOPS (memory-bound). Tier-1 and Tier-2 workloads separate by 56$\times$ in median operational intensity with no IQR overlap (Fig.~\ref{fig:quadrant}), and MoE models execute 63\% more kernels per forward pass than dense models.
    \item \textbf{Per-operator latency predictors that compose to end-to-end.} We fit per-kernel XGBoost predictors on shape-derivable features alone, with no runtime counters, and compose them analytically into single-device E2E latency (Sec.~\ref{sec:predictor}). This extends the analytical operator-latency predictors of LLMCompass~\citep{llmcompass} and Vidur~\citep{vidur} to shape-only features and held-out architectures. LLMCompass and Vidur use runtime or counter-derived features; our predictor requires only shape. On two models never seen during training (Qwen-2.5-72B and Llama-3.3-70B), the composed prediction matches measured single-layer latency with mean absolute error of 4.03\% (Qwen-2.5-72B $-7.71$\%, Llama-3.3-70B $+0.35$\%), enabling latency estimation for new architectures without re-running GPU experiments.
\end{enumerate}
