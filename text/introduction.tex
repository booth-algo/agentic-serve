\textit{How should we benchmark LLM inference when the dominant workload is a multi-step coding agent, not a short chat exchange?} LLM agents now power coding assistants and computer-use systems. Inference benchmarks still evaluate serving infrastructure with chat-scale single-turn traces, arbitrary synthetic lengths, and fixed operating points. The MLPerf Inference v5.1 LLM suite~\citep{mlperf_v51} covers summarisation and long-context reasoning, with workloads from 1{,}024-token Q\&A (Llama~2~70B on OpenOrca) up to 128K-token document tasks (Llama~3.1~405B). It remains single-turn with no multi-step tool use. The harnesses bundled with Kwon \etal{}'s vLLM~\citep{vllm} and Zheng \etal{}'s SGLang~\citep{sglang} default to ShareGPT~\citep{sharegpt} prompts clustered near 1{,}024 tokens at a fixed operating point (one in-flight-request level, held constant across the run).

Benchmarking has three gaps. First, no public benchmark measures the agentic, multi-turn, tool-using regime that now dominates production: coding and computer-use agents issue long-context multi-turn requests that neither MLPerf, vLLM, nor SGLang exercises. Second, ISL and OSL (input/output length) are set by the benchmark author, not measured from a trace: InferenceX~\citep{inferencex} treats them as user-supplied knobs; its random-token profiles match requested counts but not trace distributions. Third, every public serving-benchmark harness reports a single operating point: MLPerf Server~\citep{mlperf} fixes open-loop Poisson arrivals (arrivals drawn at a fixed rate, independent of completions) at a pre-chosen rate, vLLM and SGLang default to a fixed in-flight-request level, and none sweeps the full load curve to find where serving capacity ceases to rise.

Three terms frame the benchmark. \textit{Closed-loop concurrency} ($C$) is the number of requests held in flight by an admission gate that admits the next request only when one completes. It matches production agent traffic, where the next request is gated by the previous completion in a tool-use loop, more faithfully than open-loop Poisson. \textit{Request throughput} ($\,$req/s) is \textsc{AgenticServe}'s headline capacity metric. We prefer it to \textit{output token throughput} ($\,$tok/s), which hides the agentic gap: at 21:1 ISL/OSL ratios each request demands a long prefill but produces few output tokens, so tok/s remains high while req/s drops. We also prefer it to \textit{total token throughput}, which conflates prefill-bound input with decode-bound output. \textit{Saturation} is the concurrency $C^{\star}\,{=}\,\argmax_{C}\,\text{req/s}(C)$ at which request throughput ceases to rise with added concurrency (slope-floor $|d(\text{req/s})/dC|\,{<}\,\epsilon$). At $C^{\star}$, the serving pipeline itself is the bottleneck: KV-cache, prefill GEMMs, and scheduler queues~\citep{sarathi} admit no further concurrency, and adding requests only grows the queue. We prefer $C^{\star}$ to peak$\,$tok/s, which synthetic workloads inflate by reporting decoded tokens without saturating the pipeline. The time-to-first-token (TTFT) knee and KV-cache fill fraction corroborate $C^{\star}$ but do not replace it.

Two quantities locate a workload on the roofline. \textit{Operational intensity} ($\text{OI} = 2mdLB / (md + dLB + mLB)$, FLOPs per byte of DRAM traffic) captures whether a (model, batch, sequence-length) configuration is compute-bound or memory-bound. \textit{Capacity footprint} ($\text{CF} = m\,b_p / \text{TP} + B \cdot 2dL(n_{\text{kv}}/n_h)\,b_{\text{kv}} / \text{TP}$, per-GPU GB) is the weight-plus-KV-cache memory a configuration consumes; when CF approaches HBM capacity, the memory wall binds. Both are derived in Sec.~\ref{sec:method}.

In this paper, we present \textsc{AgenticServe}, a benchmark suite and analysis framework that addresses each of these problems. We organise workloads into three tiers. Our Tier-1 profiles are drawn from real agentic traces: SWE-Bench~\citep{swebench} coding agents (ISL\,$\approx$\,17{,}000 / OSL\,$\approx$\,800, 21.3:1 ratio) and TerminalBench and OSWorld multi-turn sessions of 2 to 1{,}000 turns. ShareGPT single- and multi-turn chat serve as Tier-2 baselines. We measure ISL and OSL distributions from these traces directly. Synthetic random-token profiles (decode-heavy, prefill-heavy, random-1k) are classified as Tier-3 microbenchmarks that isolate a single regime rather than stand in for a deployed workload. We sweep closed-loop concurrency from $C\,{=}\,1$ to $320$ to locate $C^{\star}$ for every (model, profile) pair.

Consider Llama-3.1-8B on an H100 at saturation. Under chat-short, it serves 55.15\,req/s at 282\,ms TTFT. Under coding-agent, req/s drops to 15.03 and TTFT rises to 598\,ms: a 3.7$\times$ capacity reduction alongside a 2.1$\times$ latency increase. Across the five models with coding-agent data, TTFT is 1.8--5.6$\times$ longer than chat at saturation (Table~\ref{tab:serving-perf}). Multi-turn sessions compound the gap. SWE-Bench agents reach 2{,}375\,ms TTFT at 4.74\,req/s, versus 498\,ms at 19.37\,req/s for chat multi-turn on the same model (Table~\ref{tab:multi-turn}): TTFT increases 4.8$\times$ while throughput drops 4.1$\times$. In every case, the metric that matters for deployed capacity, req/s at saturation, degrades more than latency alone suggests. Tier-1 and Tier-2 workloads separate by 56$\times$ in median operational intensity with no IQR overlap (Sec.~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline}), a regime shift, not a marginal increase.

Figure~\ref{fig:roofline} anchors on Llama-3.1-70B chat-long ($\text{TP}=2$, per-GPU), concurrency sweeping from 1 to 320, with two companion chat profiles. Each panel stacks three curves in a roofline cascade. The dashed line is the H100 hardware ceiling. The green line is the workload-achievable ceiling, time-weighted from the per-kernel peaks in Sec.~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline}. The red lines are what vLLM actually delivers across the three measured chat profiles. The kernel ceiling sits about $3.5\times$ below hardware, and the serving pipeline sits another ${\sim}130\times$ below the kernel ceiling on both axes. The kernels are not the binding constraint; the serving pipeline is.

\textsc{AgenticServe} makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Saturation benchmarks on real agentic workloads.} We sweep closed-loop concurrency from 1 to 320 across six models spanning four families (Llama, Qwen, Mixtral, GPT-OSS; dense and mixture-of-experts (MoE), 8B--120B total parameters) on H100 and A100 GPUs under vLLM, collecting 2{,}527 benchmark configurations (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}). Agentic workloads increase TTFT by 1.8--5.6$\times$ single-turn and up to 4.8$\times$ multi-turn, and cut request throughput by up to 3.7$\times$ against chat baselines.
    \item \textbf{Workload- and kernel-level roofline with OI/CF characterisation.} Figure~\ref{fig:roofline} quantifies a hardware-to-kernel-to-serving cascade: hardware sits $3.5\times$ above the kernel-composition ceiling (Sec.~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline}), which itself sits ${\sim}130\times$ above observed serving throughput on both operational intensity and capacity footprint. We profile $\sim$3{,}000 CUDA kernel invocations across 48 model-phase-batch configurations on a kernel-level roofline (Sec.~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline}). Prefill GEMMs reach 785\,TFLOPS on H100 (compute-bound); decode GEMMs operate at 2.9\,TFLOPS (memory-bound). Tier-1 and Tier-2 workloads separate by 56$\times$ in median operational intensity with no IQR overlap (MoE models execute 63\% more kernels per forward pass than dense).
    \item \textbf{Per-operator latency predictors that compose to end-to-end.} We fit per-kernel XGBoost predictors on shape-derivable features alone, with no runtime counters, and compose them analytically into single-device E2E latency (Sec.~\ref{sec:predictor}). This extends the analytical operator-latency predictors of LLMCompass~\citep{llmcompass} and Vidur~\citep{vidur} to shape-only features and held-out architectures. LLMCompass and Vidur use runtime or counter-derived features; our predictor requires only shape. On two models never seen during training (Qwen-2.5-72B and Llama-3.3-70B), the composed prediction matches measured single-layer latency with mean absolute error of 4.03\% (Qwen-2.5-72B $-7.71$\%, Llama-3.3-70B $+0.35$\%), enabling latency estimation for new architectures without re-running GPU experiments.
\end{enumerate}
