\textit{How should we benchmark LLM inference when the dominant workload is no longer a short chat exchange but a multi-step coding agent that sends tens of thousands of tokens per request?} LLM agents now power coding assistants, terminal automation, and computer-use systems, yet inference benchmarks continue to evaluate serving infrastructure with chat-scale single-turn traces, arbitrary synthetic lengths, and fixed operating points. The MLPerf Inference v5.1 LLM suite~\citep{mlperf_v51} evaluates summarisation, chat, and long-context reasoning, with workloads ranging from 1{,}024-token Q\&A (Llama~2~70B on OpenOrca) to 128K-token document tasks (Llama~3.1~405B), but remains single-turn with no multi-step tool use. The harnesses bundled with Kwon \etal{}'s vLLM~\citep{vllm} and Zheng \etal{}'s SGLang~\citep{sglang} default to ShareGPT~\citep{sharegpt} prompts whose input lengths cluster around 1{,}024 tokens at a fixed concurrency.

We identify three problems with this status quo. First, no existing benchmark evaluates the agentic, multi-turn, tool-using regime that now dominates production: coding agents, terminal agents, and computer-use agents all issue long-context multi-turn requests that neither MLPerf, vLLM, nor SGLang measures. Second, synthetic and chat-scale input/output length (ISL/OSL) distributions are set arbitrarily by the benchmark author rather than measured from a real trace; InferenceX~\citep{inferencex} treats ISL, OSL, and concurrency as user-supplied parameters without trace grounding, and its synthetic random-token profiles reproduce the requested token counts but not the KV-cache pressure, prefill GEMM contention, or scheduler queuing~\citep{sarathi} of a real agent trace. Third, the three widely-used public serving-benchmark harnesses each report a single operating point: MLPerf Server~\citep{mlperf} fixes open-loop Poisson arrival at a pre-chosen rate, and vLLM and SGLang default to a fixed concurrency --- none sweeps closed-loop concurrency to saturation.

In this paper, we present \textsc{AgentServe}, a benchmark suite and analysis framework that addresses each of these problems directly. To ground the workload mix, \textsc{AgentServe}'s Tier-1 profiles are drawn from real agentic traces --- SWE-Bench~\citep{swebench} coding agents (ISL\,$\approx$\,17{,}000 / OSL\,$\approx$\,800, 21.3:1 ratio), TerminalBench and OSWorld multi-turn sessions of 2 to 1{,}000 turns --- with ShareGPT single- and multi-turn chat as Tier-2 baselines. To replace arbitrary ISL/OSL knobs, we measure both distributions from real agentic and chat traces and restrict synthetic random-token profiles (decode-heavy, prefill-heavy, random-1k) to Tier-3 microbenchmarks that isolate a single regime (prefill-bound, decode-bound, or balanced) rather than stand in for a deployed workload. To expose the saturation ceiling, we run a \textit{closed-loop concurrency sweep} from $C\,{=}\,1$ to $320$ on A100 and H100 using an \texttt{asyncio.Semaphore(C)} admission gate: $C$ requests are in flight at once, and the next is admitted only when one completes. Closed-loop concurrency matches production agent traffic --- where the next request is gated by the previous completion in a tool-use loop --- more faithfully than open-loop Poisson, which assumes independent arrivals.

We define three terms explicitly. \textit{Concurrency} is the maximum number of in-flight requests under this closed-loop gate, in contrast to the open-loop Poisson arrival used in MLPerf Server. \textit{Request throughput} ($\,$req/s) is \textsc{AgentServe}'s headline capacity metric --- not \textit{output token throughput} ($\,$tok/s), which undersells the agentic gap at 21:1 ISL/OSL ratios, and not \textit{total token throughput}, which conflates prefill-bound input with decode-bound output. \textit{Saturation} is the concurrency $C^{\star}\,{=}\,\argmax_{C}\,\text{req/s}(C)$ at which request throughput ceases to rise with added concurrency (slope-floor $|d(\text{req/s})/dC|\,{<}\,\epsilon$); we prefer this over peak$\,$tok/s because the latter is inflated by synthetic workloads that do not saturate the serving pipeline. The TTFT-knee and KV-cache fill fraction corroborate $C^{\star}$ but do not replace it.

To illustrate, Llama-3.1-8B on an H100 sustains 9{,}327\,tok/s of output under a chat-short workload but only 4{,}904\,tok/s under a coding-agent workload at saturation, a 1.9$\times$ gap; the capacity gap is larger still, at 55.15\,req/s versus 15.03\,req/s, a 3.7$\times$ reduction in requests served per second. TTFT widens further: single-turn agentic requests incur 26--468$\times$ longer time-to-first-token than chat, and multi-turn sessions 5--28$\times$, across configurations. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:quadrant}, Tier-1 and Tier-2 separate by 56$\times$ in median operational intensity with no IQR overlap --- a qualitative regime shift, not a quantitative nudge.

\textsc{AgentServe} makes three contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Saturation benchmarks on real agentic workloads.} We sweep closed-loop concurrency from 1 to 320 across six models spanning three families (Llama-3, Qwen3.5, GPT-OSS; dense and MoE, 8B--120B total parameters) on H100 and A100 GPUs under vLLM, collecting 2{,}527 benchmark configurations (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}). Agentic workloads raise TTFT by 26--468$\times$ single-turn and 5--28$\times$ multi-turn, and cut request throughput by up to 3.7$\times$ against chat baselines.
    \item \textbf{Kernel-level roofline with OI/CF characterisation.} We profile $\sim$3{,}000 CUDA kernel invocations across 48 model-phase-batch configurations (Sec.~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline}). Prefill GEMMs reach up to 785\,TFLOPS on H100 (compute-bound); decode GEMMs operate at 2.9\,TFLOPS (memory-bound). Tier-1 and Tier-2 workloads separate by 56$\times$ in median operational intensity with no IQR overlap (Fig.~\ref{fig:quadrant}), and MoE models execute 63\% more kernels per forward pass than dense models.
    \item \textbf{Per-operator latency predictors that compose to end-to-end.} We fit per-kernel XGBoost predictors on shape-derivable features alone --- no runtime counters --- and compose them analytically into single-device E2E latency (Sec.~\ref{sec:predictor}), extending the analytical operator-latency predictors of LLMCompass~\citep{llmcompass} and Vidur~\citep{vidur} to shape-only features and held-out architectures. LLMCompass and Vidur use runtime or counter-derived features; our predictor requires only shape. On two models never seen during training (Qwen-2.5-72B and Llama-3.3-70B), the composed prediction matches measured single-layer latency with mean absolute error of 4.03\% (Qwen-2.5-72B $-7.71$\%, Llama-3.3-70B $+0.35$\%), enabling latency estimation for new architectures without re-running GPU experiments.
\end{enumerate}
