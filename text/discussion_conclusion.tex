\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Capacity planning.}
Chat benchmarks overestimate serving capacity for agentic workloads by up to 3.8$\times$ (Section~\ref{sec:workload_gap}). A deployment sized from chat-benchmark numbers will either overprovision decode-side resources (memory bandwidth) or underprovision prefill compute, leading to TTFT violations under agentic load. Prefill-decode disaggregation~\citep{distserve, splitwise} is one mitigation---our data quantifies where the split should occur.

\paragraph{Tensor parallelism.}
TP decisions interact with workload profile. For decode-heavy chat workloads, increasing TP adds allreduce overhead with diminishing latency benefit. For prefill-heavy agentic workloads, TP distributes compute-bound GEMMs across GPUs more effectively. Practitioners should evaluate TP configurations against their actual workload distribution, not a generic chat benchmark.

\paragraph{Multi-turn context growth.}
Agent sessions accumulate context across turns, growing KV cache and prefill cost. This makes KV cache-aware scheduling and context-length-dependent routing increasingly important as agents move from single-turn to session-based interaction patterns.

\paragraph{Why kernel-level analysis.}
The roofline analysis in Section~\ref{sec:kernel_roofline} makes the throughput gap actionable: the bottleneck for agentic workloads is prefill GEMM throughput, not decode batching efficiency. This points to chunked prefill, speculative prefill, or dedicated prefill GPUs as the high-leverage optimizations for agentic serving.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
(1)~We evaluate two GPU generations (A100, H100); newer hardware (B200, MI300X) may shift the compute--memory balance.
(2)~All experiments are single-node; multi-node deployments introduce communication bottlenecks we do not capture.
(3)~ncu profiles are from 2-layer model stubs, which may underestimate cache effects at full depth.
(4)~We do not evaluate quantized models (FP8, INT4), which alter both roofline characteristics and serving throughput.
(5)~Our agentic traces come from software engineering agents; web browsing, data analysis, and tool-orchestration agents may exhibit different ISL/OSL distributions.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Two extensions follow directly: (1)~benchmarking prefill-decode disaggregated serving~\citep{distserve, splitwise} under our workload taxonomy, where the workload-dependent bottleneck analysis informs placement decisions; and (2)~building a kernel-level simulator that predicts serving metrics from ncu data, enabling co-design without full GPU experiments.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Agentic LLM workloads differ from chat workloads in a measurable, consequential way: ISL/OSL ratios above 20:1 shift GPU utilization from memory-bound decode to compute-bound prefill, reducing peak serving throughput by up to 3.8$\times$. Current inference benchmarks do not test this regime. \textsc{AgentServe-Bench} fills the gap with real agent traces, systematic saturation analysis, and kernel-level roofline data that explains the throughput difference mechanistically. We release the full dataset and tooling to support reproducible evaluation of inference systems under realistic agentic workloads.
